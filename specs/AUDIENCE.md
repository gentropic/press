# AUDIENCE.md — be honest about who a book is for; grade the series

A house convention, written after the first real-reader review of *Field Kit
No.01* (from Claudia Endlein — the author's mother — June 2026). The lesson:

> **A poetic surface does not lower the conceptual bar. Name the true audience,
> and if a book reaches for too wide a range, split it into a graded series rather
> than pretend one book serves all ages.**

## What the review found (and why it's trustworthy)

Two independent reviewers — a first-time reader and the in-house critique — landed
on the *same* point with no contact between them. That convergence means it is a
real property of the book, not a matter of taste:

- The **subtitle claims** "for the very young." True for Acts I–II (the ground, it
  varies, we drill). But by the variogram / kriging / simulation half it is really
  *"for the grown-up reading aloud, with the child watching the pictures."*
- The reviewer's exact words: *"Não é o assunto, é a forma de explicar. Será que
  uma criança vai entender o conceito de curvas?"* — i.e. the **topic** is
  kid-accessible; specific **abstract concepts** (a difference-vs-distance scatter,
  "draw the curve") are 8th/9th-grade material however poetically phrased.
- The praise is also a finding to **protect**: *"parece poesia, meio ziraldiniana"*
  — the two-voice read-aloud register genuinely works as children's poetry. Don't
  lose it while fixing the level.

## The constructive direction (don't dumb down; grade the series)

Options weighed:
1. **Re-label No.01's audience** ("for the curious, 9 to 99") — honest, zero work,
   but abandons the little-kids dream.
2. **Dumb down No.01** — risks gutting the elegant minimalism that makes it good;
   fights the book's nature.
3. **Add a FieldKit No.00 underneath it** — *chosen.* Resolves the tension WITHOUT
   touching the finished No.01. The series becomes graded:
   - **No.00 = observation** — truly for the very young: noticing, comparing, "the
     ground is layered and it varies, if you look." Concrete, sensory, hands-on,
     **no curve required.**
   - **No.01 = geostatistics** — the next step up; stops pretending to be for
     4-year-olds and becomes the honest "level 2" that No.00 has prepared them for.

So No.01 didn't fail — it **found its real audience (older kids + curious adults)
and revealed a foundation book beneath it.** The series gained a floor.

## Design principles for the very-young tier (No.00 and future kid-facing books)

From the reviewer, and on-brand for GCU:
- **Baixar o nível: concept through concrete comparison.** Kids reach abstraction
  by comparing two real things, not by reading a plotted relationship.
- **Propor experiências — make the reader DO something.** This is the GCU spine:
  playback you *play*, ELIZA you *run*, FieldKit No.00 you should *observe / try*.
  A kid-book in this house is an instrument, not a lecture.
- **Anchor in a remembered, sensory moment.** The review handed us No.00's opening
  image: *Tante Christel showing the soil layers in the road-cut hillsides —
  "nunca esqueci."* The ground is layered, it varies, you can see it — the whole
  thesis of geology-for-kids in one concrete memory, zero math.
- **The Trojan-horse goal (handle with care):** smuggle genuinely useful habits of
  mind — observe, compare, notice variation, ask "why here and not there" — into a
  book a child reads for delight. Never at the cost of the delight; the tools ride
  *under* the story, they don't replace it.

## The developmental ladder — which data skill lands at which rung

So "baixar o nível" is a *target*, not a vibe. Ages are rough, overlapping, and
scaffolding-dependent (the old "not until 11" Piagetian picture is largely revised
— kids do far more, far earlier, *when the abstraction is concretely scaffolded*).
Sanity-check specifics against current ed research; the shape is well-established.

**The governing principle: Concrete → Pictorial → Abstract** (Bruner). The *same*
concept is reachable years earlier at the concrete end than the abstract end. The
variogram isn't hard because "difference vs. distance" is hard — it's hard because
it sits at the top (abstract) rung. Move a concept down a rung and the age drops.

| Age (rough) | Rung | Data skills genuinely reachable |
|---|---|---|
| **3–5** | concrete | sort/classify (the root of all analysis); compare (more/less, same/different); count; **object "graphs"** — a bar chart made of *real things* lined up in rows. Graph-*reading* is present at 4; it's just not on paper. |
| **5–7** | pictorial begins | pictographs (one drawing = one thing) → simple bar charts (a bar *stands for* the things); **tally tables** as records of observation; read *off* a graph ("tallest bar = dogs"). |
| **7–9** | reading *between* | compare within a graph ("3 more than"), totals; the axis as a *measured scale*, not just labels. |
| **9–11+** | reading *beyond* | **line graphs / continuous relationships ("curves")**; scatter, trend, "what the shape *means*"; infer/predict. ← No.01's variogram lives here. |

**Curcio's three levels of graph comprehension** (the same idea, sharper):
1. **Read the data** (what's literally there) — ~5–6
2. **Read between the data** (compare, combine) — ~7–9
3. **Read beyond the data** (infer, predict, trend) — ~10+

A child is fluent at level 1 years before level 3. **No.01 demands level 3** (infer
a smooth curve from scattered points, predict the unsampled) — that is exactly the
mismatch the review caught.

### What this means for No.00 (target the bottom rung deliberately)
- Live in the **concrete rung**: sort, compare, notice variation, tally with real
  marks. Teach the *attitude* of data ("let's count and compare and see") — fully
  doable at 4 — with **zero axes**.
- The deepest analysis habits aren't graphs at all: **categorize, compare, notice
  variation, record what you see, ask "is this always true?"** Teachable at 3–4,
  purely concretely, and they're precisely what makes a graph *understandable*
  later. The graph is just the eventual *notation* for habits a preschooler can
  already practice with a handful of stones.
- **The earliest honest "graph" (~age 4):** have the child collect a few rocks and
  line them up pale→dark. That's *ordering by a variable* — a real axis — built
  from objects in the hand. Nothing is drawn, yet they've *made* a graph. A spread
  like this is the No.00 sweet spot: it plants No.01's "spatial variability" and
  "near things are alike" as pure play.

## The general rule for any GCU book

State the true audience plainly (cover, subtitle, colophon). If you feel a book
straining to span toddler-to-adult, that strain is the signal to **grade** — make
the floor a separate, simpler, more concrete book — not to compromise either end
in one volume. A graded series is honest; a one-size book that fits no one is not.
